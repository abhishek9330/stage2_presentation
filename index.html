§ 01 / Abstract

A measurement problem, reframed.

Early childhood is a period of rapid cognitive, motor, and social development, and reliable measurement of these developmental trajectories is essential for identifying children at risk and informing early intervention. Existing assessment tools combine rich item-level response data with expert-designed scoring rubrics — clinically grounded, but unlikely to fully exploit the latent structure of the responses, and demanding extensive re-adaptation across cultural contexts.

This report develops a data-driven framework for measuring child development directly from item-level responses. The data come from the STREAM initiative across three instruments — MDAT, DEEP, and START — yielding roughly 4,000 subjects across approximately 1,000 features, with the vast majority of children exhibiting prototypically neurotypical development.

Move from rubric-defined scores to a latent representation that the data itself reveals.

We propose an unsupervised probabilistic generative model that combines Item Response Theory (IRT) with a Variational Autoencoder. An encoder maps high-dimensional response patterns into a low-dimensional latent ability space; a decoder is constrained to a 2PL IRT model for binary items and a Graded Response Model for ordinal ones. Missingness is handled natively through a masking mechanism on the reconstruction loss — and the whole thing trains quickly on commodity hardware.

§ 02 / Approach

An IRT-constrained VAE.

Classical IRT recovers a single latent ability per child, but struggles to scale to thousands of heterogeneous items spread across instruments. A VAE scales effortlessly, but its decoder is a black box that resists psychometric interpretation. We compose the two: a neural encoder for flexibility, an IRT decoder for interpretability.

— 01

Item responses

Binary and ordinal responses across MDAT, DEEP and START — pooled, masked for missingness.

— 02

VAE encoder

Maps each child's response pattern into a low-dimensional latent ability vector with a posterior distribution.

— 03

IRT decoder

2PL for binary items, Graded Response Model for ordinal items — psychometric structure preserved.

— 04

Latent ability

A continuous, instrument-agnostic developmental score per child — the object we then validate.

The result is a model that respects decades of psychometric theory while letting the data choose the representation. Training is fast, the reconstruction loss is masked to handle missing items natively, and the latent space is small enough to interpret and visualise.

§ 03 / Validation

Three axes of evidence.

— Axis 01

Tracks chronological age

The learned latent measures are strongly monotonic in age, confirming that the model recovers genuine developmental progress rather than artefacts of the response space.

— Axis 02

Aligns with risk & protective factors

After age-adjustment via GAMLSS, the scores correlate in expected directions with HAZ, SES, FCI, MORS, CPRS and the RBSK risk indicator — sensitive to adversity and protection alike.

— Axis 03

Recovers clinical scores

A thin neural network maps latent representations to the Griffiths Mental Development Scales (GMDS), establishing criterion validity against an established clinical instrument.

§ 04 / Deployment

From model to field.

An age-staged strategy, aligned to WHO GSED.

We propose pairing the right instrument with the right developmental window — motivated by training cost, age-appropriateness, and alignment with the WHO Global Scales for Early Development.

Ages 0 – 3
MDAT
Malawi Developmental Assessment Tool — the primary scoring instrument for the earliest years.
Ages 3 – 6
DEEP
Developmental Assessment on an E-Platform — age-appropriate items for older toddlers and preschoolers.
Cross-cutting
START
Treated separately as a screening rather than scoring tool — for autism-risk flagging, not developmental quantification.
